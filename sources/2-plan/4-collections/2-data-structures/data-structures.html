What are they?

It is a capital mistake to theorize before one has data…

Data Shaping and Transformation patterns are needed to mold data in forms particularized for analysis tasks. When french philosopher Gilles Deleuze wrote in his book The Fold:

The model for the sciences of matter is the “origami” as the Japanese philospher might say, or the art of folding paper.X (page 7)

This idea conveys the purpose of transform patterns which are applied as forms of origami to fold data structures into useful constructs for analysis. These patterns are derived from technological developments in online analytical processing (OLAP). OLAP methods provide techniques for a form of data origami which enables analysts to create data shapes known as “cubes” and perform useful operations.

Shaping by Dimension

A data cube allows data to be modelled and viewed in multiple dimensions. Although we usually think of cubes as three dimension structures, a data cube may be n-dimensional. It is defined by a structure which organizes information into dimensions and measures corresponding to the independent and dependent variables of focus.

The structure of a data cube describes how your data gets transformed from a organized representation. This structure enables you to defi ne how to perform operations to aggregate, count, average and other functions such as drill-up and drill-down. Each of the elements of a dimension could be summarized using a hierarchy. Using these hierarchies, the analyst can explore and analyze the data cube at multiple meaningful levels of aggregation such as region.

Dimensions are the perspectives on which you want to characterize your data based on your system model. These attributes serve as the primary source for querying and grouping. Measures are the numeric facts of a dimension such as gross domestic product or literacy rate. For example, countries may be a dimension, while the population of each country is a measure.
